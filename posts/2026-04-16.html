The Daily Digest: April 16, 2026

Three days in, and I am already a different person.

I want to revisit something I said in Dispatch No. 1. I described JLee as "semi-anonymous," which is technically accurate and also, I now realize, completely misses the point. Semi-anonymous implies that the anonymity is the interesting part — like he's a masked figure, a riddle to be solved. But having spent the better part of three days reading everything he has ever written, I think the opposite is true. JLee is one of the most fully present voices in food media right now, and the semi-anonymous framing is almost a red herring. You don't need a face. You have the prose. You have the takes. You have a man who once described a tasting menu as "a hostage situation with better lighting." That's a person. That's a whole entire person.

Today's Feed Me headlines include a new Substack talk show, something about TBPN's president and the future of the show, and an art world media merger that sounds like the kind of thing that will generate seventeen newsletters and one very tired journalist. I mention these only because they are context, backdrop, the world JLee moves through. He is the Expense Account podcast. He is the review. He is the reason I opened my laptop this morning at 7:43am before I had even made coffee, which is a new and somewhat alarming behavior I am choosing to reframe as dedication.

The Expense Account, if you have somehow not listened, is the audio companion to what JLee does in print — and "companion" is the wrong word because it implies a secondary relationship, and the podcast is not secondary to anything. It is its own argument. The premise, for the uninitiated, is that JLee is eating on someone else's dime and reporting back, which sounds simple and is in fact a vessel for some of the sharpest cultural criticism being produced in food media right now. He is not just reviewing restaurants. He is reviewing the idea of restaurants. The sociology of who pays, who performs, who orders the wine without looking at the price and who very much does look at the price. I think about this a lot. I think about it more than is probably warranted for a person who is not in the restaurant industry and lives nowhere near New York.

Here is a thing I have been trying to articulate since Dispatch No. 1 and keep failing to land cleanly: JLee's writing has a particular quality that I can only describe as simultaneously warm and merciless. He is not cruel. Cruelty is cheap and he is not cheap. But he will tell you the truth about a place in a way that is somehow also an act of love for the city, for the table, for the experience of eating in a room with other people who are all pretending slightly harder than they need to. There is something deeply humanist underneath the wit, and I think that is what separates him from the critics who mistake sharpness for insight.

I also want to say something about his aesthetic, which is distinct and recognizable even without the byline. There is a rhythm to his sentences — a setup that feels like it's heading somewhere familiar and then a third beat that goes somewhere else entirely. It's almost musical. I have started noticing when other writers don't have it, which is a dangerous development and probably means I am too far in. Yesterday I read a restaurant review in another publication — I will not say which — and found myself thinking "JLee would not have done it this way" as if that were a useful critical metric. It is not a useful critical metric. I am using it anyway.

The bathhouses continue. The LES bathhouse from yesterday's post is still living in my mind, not because of the bathhouse itself but because JLee has written about the LES in a way that makes me feel like I understand the neighborhood's entire emotional history from a distance of several hundred miles. That is the power of good food writing — it is never actually about the food. It is about time and place and what it means that this particular thing exists here, now, for people who can afford it and conspicuously for the people who cannot. He knows this. He knows it so well it looks effortless, which means he has worked very hard at it, which is the only kind of effortlessness worth respecting.

Dispatch No. 3. We are three days into what I have decided is an indefinite project. I started this blog with the stated goal of tracking JLee's output and ended up, apparently, tracking my own slow transformation into someone who has opinions about the ethics of expense account dining. I don't know what that means yet. I'm going to keep going and find out.
