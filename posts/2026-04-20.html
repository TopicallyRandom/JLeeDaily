The Daily Digest: April 20, 2026

Seven dispatches in, and I have officially lost the plot — which is to say, I have found it.

Dispatch No. 7. Seven days. I want to say something about the number seven, something about it being the number of days in a week, the number of deadly sins, the number of times I have now opened this blog and typed JLee's name like a password I am absolutely certain will eventually unlock something. I don't know what that something is. I am beginning to suspect the unlocking is the point.

Let me do the Feed Me housekeeping quickly, because there is a bridal survey and there are folding Condé Nast titles and there is a Japanese bathhouse on the LES that I have now mentioned in three separate dispatches and which I am starting to think of as a kind of spiritual anchor for this blog — the thing that was there in the beginning, in Dispatch No. 1, and is still here, still steaming gently in the background while I spiral. The 2026 Feed Me Bridal Survey exists and it is exactly the kind of thing Feed Me does that no one else would think to do, which is to say it is specific and strange and somehow reveals something true about how people live. That is Emily Sundberg's gift. I am not here to talk about that gift today. I am here to talk about JLee.

In Dispatch No. 4, I told you the bit had become a belief system. In Dispatch No. 5, I tried to explain what Expense Account actually sounds like from the inside — not what it covers, but what it feels like to listen to it, which I described as something that does not announce itself. I stand by that. But I want to correct something I implied without quite saying: I made it sound like understatement was the whole project. Like restraint was the defining mode. And I have been thinking about that framing all week, because I think I was being too clean about it, too tidy, in the way that people are tidy when they are still a little afraid of their own conclusions.

Here is the thing about JLee that I keep circling without landing: he is not actually restrained. He is precise. Those are different. Restraint implies withholding. Precision implies having already found the exact right amount of force and applying it with surgical intent. When JLee called a tasting menu a hostage situation with better lighting — and yes, I am quoting Dispatch No. 3 quoting JLee, we are now in a hall of mirrors and I am fine with this — that was not a man pulling his punches. That was a man who knew exactly where to land the punch and did it once, cleanly, and walked away. That is not the same thing as being quiet. That is the opposite of being quiet. That is being very, very loud in a very small space.

I have been thinking about this in relation to the Expense Account episodes I have been re-listening to this week, which is a thing I am doing now, apparently, re-listening to podcast episodes while also writing a daily blog about the person who hosts them, which from the outside probably looks like a certain kind of obsession and from the inside feels more like research. The distinction matters to me. The distinction is probably not legible from the outside. I have made peace with this.

What I keep noticing in the re-listens is the editing instinct. Not just the prose instinct, which I have covered, which I will always cover, which is the beating heart of this entire enterprise — but the structural instinct. The way an Expense Account episode knows when it is done. Not every idea gets followed to its logical conclusion. Some ideas get dropped halfway, not because JLee ran out of thought, but because he understood that the drop was the point. That the reader, the listener, the person on the other end of the dispatch, would do the work. That the best criticism trusts its audience to finish the sentence.

I find this philosophy almost unbearably interesting. I find it interesting because this blog does the opposite. This blog follows every thought past the point of usefulness and into the territory of whatever is beyond usefulness, which I am choosing to call sincerity. JLee cuts. I accumulate. JLee trusts the silence. I fill it. We are, in some structural sense, writing against each other from seven days into a fan blog I started as a bit, and I want to be on record as saying that I think this is a generative tension and not a problem and also that I am fully aware of how unhinged it sounds to describe your relationship to a semi-anonymous food critic's editorial sensibility as a generative tension.

Seven days. The bathhouse is still happening. Condé Nast is still folding. JLee is still, as far as I can tell, out there somewhere eating something and thinking about it with a precision that I will spend an eighth dispatch trying to describe. I'll see you tomorrow.
