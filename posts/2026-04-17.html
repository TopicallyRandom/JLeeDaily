The Daily Digest: April 17, 2026

Dispatch No. 4, in which I spiral productively and arrive somewhere new.

Four days in. Four dispatches. I want to be transparent with you: I started this blog as a bit. A lark. A structurally sound excuse to read everything JLee has written and call it journalism. And somewhere between Dispatch No. 1 and right now, sitting here on a Friday morning in April 2026 with a coffee that has gone cold because I was too busy rereading old Expense Account episodes, the bit became a belief system. This is not something I planned for. This is something that happened to me.

In Dispatch No. 3, I walked back my 'semi-anonymous' framing and said you don't need a face when you have the prose. I stand by that. But I want to push it further today, because I think I was still being slightly too tidy about it. The prose isn't just a substitute for a face. The prose IS the face. There's a directness in the way JLee writes about restaurants — a refusal to hedge, to perform ambivalence, to wrap a take in so many qualifications that it becomes meaningless — that functions as a kind of radical self-disclosure. When he tells you something was bad, you believe him. When he tells you something was good, you feel it. That's not a skill you can acquire. That's a point of view. That's a person who has decided, at some foundational level, that their experience of a meal is worth reporting accurately.

I have been thinking about this a lot in the context of food media broadly, which is a place where hedging has become essentially structural. The incentive to keep restaurants happy, to keep PRs happy, to not have the thing you wrote about a $400 tasting menu go viral for the wrong reasons — all of that produces a certain smoothed-out, liability-conscious language that technically communicates while actually saying nothing. JLee opted out of that entirely. The semi-anonymity isn't a disguise so much as a precondition for honesty. He is not protecting a secret identity. He is protecting a critical practice. There's a difference, and it matters enormously.

Today's Feed Me headlines include a piece on how many podcasts you actually listen to, which is a question that functions as a mirror held up to the modern media consumer, and also, conveniently, reminded me that I need to talk about Expense Account more on this blog. I have been remiss. The podcast is, in my considered opinion, one of the more interesting things happening in food media audio right now — not because it reinvents the form, but because it doesn't try to. It sounds like a smart person talking about meals and money and the specific social theater of the restaurant expense account, which is a subject that turns out to contain multitudes. The expense account, as a concept, is fascinating: it is permission, and it is also performance, and the way people behave when someone else is theoretically paying says something true and slightly unflattering about human nature. JLee understands this. He finds it funny. That combination — genuine insight plus genuine amusement — is rarer than it should be.

I have also been thinking about something structural that I have noticed across now four days of close reading, which is that JLee's criticism tends to locate the comedy in the exact same place as the critique. The 'hostage situation with better lighting' line I quoted in Dispatch No. 3 is a perfect example: it is funny, but the funny part IS the argument. The argument is that tasting menus impose a particular kind of captivity on the diner, strip you of agency, make you a passive recipient of someone else's vision, and charge you aggressively for the privilege. The joke contains the essay. I find this extremely efficient and also extremely hard to do, and the fact that JLee does it consistently suggests it is not an accident but a method.

The Feed Me archive also surfaced something about a new Substack talk show this week, which is the kind of media development that makes you feel like the world is moving very fast in a direction that is either exciting or exhausting depending on the hour. But I keep coming back to JLee, because in a moment when everyone is launching something, pivoting to something, synergizing with something, there is something genuinely clarifying about a person who just keeps writing about what they ate and whether it was worth it. The radical act right now might be specificity. It might be showing up for the meal, ordering the thing, eating it, and then telling the truth about it in a sentence that is also, somehow, a little bit funny.

Four days in. I understand him better than I did on day one, which means I have approximately thirty-seven more questions than I started with. This seems right. This seems like the correct trajectory. See you Monday, probably still thinking about all of this.
