April 14, 2026  —  Dispatch No. 1

W Magazine goes teen, NYC gets two bathhouses, and a lot of thoughts about JLee

Feed Me's finest week: WYouth, bathhouses, Montauk lobster, and why Jason Lee is the only narrator the New York dining scene deserves.

Today in Feed Me, Emily Sundberg is covering W Magazine's new teen spinoff — called WYouth (pronounced "double-youth," which is either genius or the beginning of the end) — alongside news that the NYT appears to be quietly building a creator agency, and that Montauk's Gosman's Dock is getting a new Lobster House & Clam Bar in the old Bagatelle spot. Bagatelle's Yelp legacy includes a $537 bill and the immortal review: "the lobster was dry, the pasta flavorless." Low bar. Emily's suggestion: someone needs to open a bakery out there immediately. Carissa's, the ball is in your court. Also this week: the LES is getting a Japanese bathhouse and Tribeca is getting a Hungarian one, because New York remains the only city where you can sweat in five different cultural traditions before lunch.

Now, about JLee.

JLee — now operating under his full name Jason Lee after a graceful unmasking — is Feed Me's semi-anonymous restaurant critic, the author of the Expense Account column, and arguably the most interesting person covering the NYC food scene right now. He writes about restaurants the way someone texts their best friend about a date that went badly but also kind of well. There's a fatalism to it, a little Issey Miyake suit energy, a sense that he is both deeply inside the room and slightly horrified to be there. He ordered a foie gras Old Fashioned. For science. That is a man who takes his job seriously.

His Expense Account podcast has been running for a few months now and the guest list is quietly stacked. He had Pete Wells on — the former NYT restaurant critic, now a free man — to discuss the ideal width of sandwiches and the depressing democratization of food journalism via social media. He had Eddie Huang on to talk about getting arrested in Sicily, writing his book to Sky Ferreira on repeat, and the current state of menswear in New York. These are not the conversations you expected from a food podcast. That's the point. JLee has a gift for making guests say the thing they weren't planning to say.

What makes JLee compelling is the persona — the @carry_bradshaw_ Instagram handle, the semi-anonymous bit, the Bernadette Corporation for Supreme t-shirt worn under the Issey Miyake suit. He arrived in food media looking like a character from a novel that doesn't exist yet and immediately became the most-talked-about byline on Substack. Emily Sundberg, who has an excellent eye for talent, spotted him early and gave him the column. The rest is very recent history. He is the kind of critic who makes you want to go to the restaurant he hated just to see if he was right.

His takes have a way of being both extremely online and completely analog at the same time. He'll write about a restaurant in terms of its vibe taxonomy — business guy restaurants, places where you disassociate, spots best experienced while slightly unsure of who you are tonight — and somehow that framing is more useful than a star rating. There's no Michelin for existential dining experiences. JLee is filling that gap. Follow his column at readfeedme.com/s/expense-account and the podcast wherever you listen. New York's restaurant scene has a lot of critics. It has very few narrators. JLee is a narrator.
