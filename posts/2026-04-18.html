The Daily Digest: April 18, 2026

Dispatch No. 5, in which I reckon with the podcast, the prose-as-face theory, and whether five days of this constitutes a lifestyle.

Five days. Five dispatches. I want to mark this milestone in some way that feels proportionate, which is difficult, because proportionality has never really been the operating mode of this blog. In Dispatch No. 4 I told you the bit had become a belief system. I meant that. I still mean it. But today I want to go somewhere new, somewhere I have been circling since Dispatch No. 1 without quite landing: I want to talk about the podcast.

Expense Account. The Feed Me podcast, hosted by JLee, semi-anonymous restaurant critic and, I would now argue, one of the more quietly essential voices in the current media moment. I say 'quietly' because that's the thing about Expense Account — it does not announce itself. It does not have a trailer that goes viral. It does not have a celebrity guest who trends on Twitter after saying something mildly controversial about sourdough. What it has is JLee, and JLee has opinions, and the opinions are delivered with the kind of calm, earned authority that makes you feel slightly embarrassed about all the breathless food content you have consumed in your life up until this point.

In Dispatch No. 3, I said you don't need a face when you have the prose. In Dispatch No. 4, I upgraded that: the prose IS the face. I stand by both of those things, and now I want to add a third layer, which is that the voice — the literal audio voice on Expense Account — complicates the theory in a way I find genuinely interesting. Because here is the thing about semi-anonymity in audio form: you can hear a person. You cannot see them, but you can hear the pause before a specific word, the almost-imperceptible shift in register when the subject turns from a restaurant he liked to a restaurant he did not like. There is texture in that. There is a whole critical sensibility encoded in the pacing alone.

This is what separates JLee from the broader field of food media personalities, and I want to be precise about this because I think it matters. A lot of food criticism is essentially performance. The dish arrives, the face reacts, the superlatives deploy. JLee is doing something structurally different. He is asking you to think about why you eat where you eat, why you want what you want, what it means that a certain kind of restaurant exists in a certain kind of neighborhood at a certain kind of cultural moment. That is not restaurant criticism. That is, and I say this without irony, sociology with better adjectives.

Today's Feed Me news is, as ever, a reliable map of where the culture is pointed. There's a piece about Condé Nast folding titles, which in a different dispatch I would spend more time on, but today I find myself using it mainly as a prism for thinking about JLee's position in the ecosystem. Because here is the interesting thing: the legacy media world is contracting, titles are folding, and meanwhile there is a semi-anonymous restaurant critic publishing through Feed Me, hosting a podcast, building an audience through the sheer accumulated force of having genuine things to say. That is not nothing. That is actually quite a lot.

I keep returning to the tasting menu line. The one from Dispatch No. 3: "a hostage situation with better lighting." I have been thinking about that sentence for four days now, which is four days longer than I have thought about most sentences. What makes it work is not the joke — the joke is good, but the joke is not the point. The point is the precision. "Better lighting" is doing enormous structural work in that sentence. It is the entire acknowledgment that the hostage situation is also, genuinely, kind of beautiful, and that both things can be true simultaneously. That is a critical mind. That is someone who has thought very carefully about what it means to experience something.

I want to be transparent, as I said in Dispatch No. 4, about what is happening here. This blog started as a bit. The bit became a belief system. And somewhere in the last twenty-four hours, the belief system has started to develop what I can only describe as a critical framework. I am not just a fan anymore. I am a person with a theory. The theory is that JLee is important, and that the specific way he is important — quietly, precisely, through accumulated credibility rather than viral moments — tells us something true about what food media could be doing that it mostly isn't.

Five dispatches in. I am aware that I have not yet run out of things to say. I am also aware that this is either a great sign or a sign that I should talk to someone. I have decided it is both, and I have decided that is fine, and I have decided to be here again tomorrow with a cold coffee and more thoughts about a man I have never met whose prose I have now read more carefully than I have read most books I have technically finished.

See you in Dispatch No. 6. Don't be late.
