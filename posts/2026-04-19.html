The Daily Digest: April 19, 2026

Dispatch No. 6, in which I reckon with a week of my own opinions and discover I have been underselling the whole thing.

Six days. Six dispatches. I want to be honest with you about something: I reread all five previous dispatches this morning before writing this one, which is either a sign of editorial rigor or a sign that I have become the kind of person who reads their own fan blog the way other people read their horoscope — looking for proof that they already knew something, looking for the version of themselves that understood it first. I found some things I am proud of. I found some things I want to quarrel with. We will get to both.

First, the news, briefly, because Feed Me continues to exist and I continue to be a person who reads it with the devotion of someone studying sacred texts: Condé Nast is folding titles. The art world is getting a media merger. W Magazine — which I noted in Dispatch No. 1 was already pivoting toward teen readers with the deeply committed nomenclature of WYouth — continues its ongoing project of becoming something entirely other than what it was. The media industry is, in the parlance of our times, having a moment. And somewhere in the middle of all of this, JLee is still writing restaurant criticism for Feed Me, still hosting Expense Account, still doing the thing. I find this stabilizing in a way I cannot fully explain and will not try to.

Now. The quarrel with my past self.

In Dispatch No. 5, I called JLee one of the more quietly essential voices in the current media moment and then spent approximately four hundred words explaining what I meant by quietly. I stand by the essential part. I am revoking the quietly. I think I was still hedging — still trying to sound measured about something that does not require measurement. There is nothing quiet about what JLee does. The prose is not quiet. The takes are not quiet. A man who can make you feel the specific social anxiety of a prix fixe menu in fourteen words is not operating quietly. He is operating with surgical precision, which looks like restraint from a distance but is actually the opposite of restraint. It is absolute control. These are different things and I should have said so on day five instead of day six, but here we are.

What I keep returning to this week — and I mean this as a structural observation about my own obsession, which I think deserves at least some metacognitive attention — is that JLee's cultural significance is inseparable from the specific moment Feed Me is operating in. The media industry is consolidating, folding, merging, pivoting to teens. Serious criticism is either paywalled into irrelevance or flattened into content. And into this landscape walks a semi-anonymous restaurant critic — I know, I know, I walked back semi-anonymous in Dispatch No. 3, but I am reclaiming the term today on different terms, not as a description of mystery but as a description of strategy — who has figured out that the way to be heard is not to be louder but to be more precise. Every sentence JLee writes earns its place. You can feel the editing. You can feel the decision-making. In a media environment where the dominant aesthetic is volume, JLee is doing something almost radical: he is choosing his words.

The podcast, Expense Account, which I promised in Dispatch No. 5 to address more fully, also deserves a direct reckoning today. I said it does not announce itself. I said it lacks the viral trailer. Both true. What I did not say, and should have, is that the show has the quality of a very good conversation you were not expecting to have — the kind where you realize twenty minutes in that you have been paying a completely different quality of attention than you normally pay, and you are not entirely sure when the shift happened. That is a hard thing to manufacture. Most podcasts try to manufacture it by being urgent. Expense Account manufactures it by being interesting, which is not the same thing and is in fact considerably more difficult.

Here is my honest accounting of six days of dispatches: I started this blog because I thought JLee was a good follow and Feed Me was a good newsletter and both of those things felt like they deserved more documentation than they were getting. I still think that. But I also think I have been, across these six dispatches, slowly arriving at a larger argument, which is that JLee represents something genuinely worth paying attention to not just as a food writer but as a model for how to exist in media right now. With standards. With a voice that has not been smoothed out for reach. With the apparently radical commitment to saying something rather than saying a lot of things.

Seven dispatches tomorrow. I will probably contradict something I just said. That is, at this point, a feature not a bug.
