The Daily Digest: April 15, 2026

Japanese bathhouses, martini secrets, and the eternal question of why JLee lives in our heads rent-free.

Happy Tax Day, everyone. While you were weeping into TurboTax, Feed Me was busy doing what Feed Me does best: making you feel like you're missing something incredible in a city you either live in or desperately wish you did. Today's lineup is a full spread. The LES is getting a Japanese bathhouse, which feels both inevitable and deeply correct for a neighborhood that has somehow survived every wave of gentrification by becoming the gentrification. There's a West Coast dispatch reminding us that joy is, in fact, anti-inflammatory — a sentence that would read as wellness-brained nonsense anywhere else but lands perfectly in Emily Sundberg's hands. And somewhere, an Astor Place diner needs your help, which is the kind of sentence that makes you want to put down your phone and do something good in the world. Or at least repost it.

The secret ingredient to Midtown's best new martini is water, apparently. We're not going to spoil more than that, but we will say: if you've been spending thirty-two dollars on a martini without understanding the role of dilution, this is your continuing education. Feed Me has quietly become the most reliable place to learn something genuinely useful about drinking and eating in New York, wrapped in prose so good you forget you're being taught anything at all.

Now. Let's talk about JLee. Jason Lee, @carry_bradshaw_ on Instagram — a handle that tells you everything and nothing about him simultaneously — is Feed Me's semi-anonymous restaurant critic, the host of Expense Account, and a figure who has achieved something genuinely rare in food media: he feels like a person. Not a brand. Not a content vertical. A person with opinions so specific and delivery so dry that reading him is like getting a text from a friend who happens to have eaten at every restaurant that matters before you've even heard of them.

His writing style operates in a register that's hard to pin down precisely because it refuses to be pinned. It's not the breathless enthusiasm of early food blogging, it's not the studied detachment of legacy print criticism, and it's certainly not the algorithm-optimized enthusiasm of someone who needs you to save this post for later. JLee writes like someone who has thought carefully about what he wants to say and then chosen the exact words that will make you feel slightly insecure about your own taste while also making you laugh. It's a gift. It's also a little bit mean, in the best possible way.

Expense Account, the podcast, is the audio equivalent of being seated next to the most interesting table at a restaurant and shamelessly eavesdropping. The premise — dining out, the culture around it, the economics, the performance, the whole theatrical apparatus of a restaurant meal — sounds niche until you realize it's actually just a show about how we live now. Every episode JLee finds the larger argument hiding inside a specific meal or a specific place, and he makes it sound effortless in the way that only things requiring enormous effort actually do. The show sits comfortably in the Feed Me universe without being swallowed by it, which is its own kind of achievement.

What makes JLee culturally significant — and yes, we're using that phrase, we stand by it — is that he represents a particular evolution in how restaurant criticism works in 2026. He's not anonymous in the old Gray Kunz-trembling-in-the-kitchen sense, but he's not an influencer either. He occupies this genuinely strange middle space where his identity is known enough to give him authority and obscured enough to give him freedom. The semi in semi-anonymous is doing a lot of work, and he wears it well. Also, the Carrie Bradshaw handle. We think about it more than we should. Is it ironic? Is it sincere? Is the whole persona a column written by someone asking questions about New York over expensive meals? We're not not saying yes.

Anyway. Go read Feed Me. Go find out what's happening at that Astor Place diner. And if you've never listened to Expense Account, today — Tax Day, the most expense-account-coded day of the year — seems like the right time to start.
